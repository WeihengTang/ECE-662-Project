%% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
This report presents a unified study of the \emph{Decomposed Convolutional
Filter} (DCF) framework across three related tasks.
\textbf{Part 1} shows that learned CNN filters occupy a remarkably
low-dimensional subspace: PCA truncation requires $K \ge 7$ of 9 components
to preserve accuracy without retraining, yet a single learned DCF atom
($K=1$) already achieves 98.96\% on MNIST, and Fourier-Bessel (FB) bases
match this performance with no data-driven atom learning.
\textbf{Part 2} applies DCF to MNIST$\to$SVHN domain adaptation.
Counterintuitively, adapting the coefficient matrices $a_k$ while keeping
atoms frozen (Exp~C, 93.3\% at $K=8$) far outperforms adapting the atoms
while keeping coefficients frozen (Exp~B, 81.2\%), revealing that atoms are
the \emph{domain-invariant} component for digit domains.
\textbf{Part 3} frames continual deblurring as a coefficient-swapping
problem: shared atoms learned on Task~0 define a universal deconvolution
dictionary, and per-task coefficient snapshots achieve zero catastrophic
forgetting (backward transfer = 0\,dB vs.\ $-13.1$\,dB for the baseline).
Together, the three parts demonstrate that the low-rank subspace structure
of CNN filters --- revealed by PCA --- enables compression, adaptation, and
continual learning within a single unified parameterisation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Part 1 --- PCA View of CNN Filters}
\label{sec:part1}

\subsection{Background: Decomposed Convolutional Filters}
\label{sec:p1_background}

Let $\W \in \R^{c' \times c \times l \times l}$ denote the weight tensor
of a convolutional layer ($c'$ output channels, $c$ input channels,
$l \times l$ spatial kernels).  Reshaping to
$\W' \in \R^{c'c \times l^2}$ reveals the \emph{filter matrix}.
Its compact SVD is
\begin{equation}
  \W' = \mathbf{U}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{V}^\top, \quad
  \mathbf{U}\in\R^{c'c\times r},\;
  \mathbf{S}=\mathrm{diag}(s_1,\ldots,s_r),\;
  \mathbf{V}\in\R^{l^2\times r}.
\end{equation}
Truncating to $K < r$ components gives the best rank-$K$ approximation
(Eckart--Young theorem).  This is exactly the DCF representation
\cite{qiu2018dcfnet}:
\begin{equation}
  \W[i,j] \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} a_k[i,j]\,\psivec_k,
  \qquad
  \psivec_k \in \R^{l \times l},\quad
  a_k \in \R^{c' \times c},
  \label{eq:dcf}
\end{equation}
where $\psivec_k = \mathbf{V}_{:,k}$ (filter atoms / principal directions)
and $a_k[i,j] = s_k\,\mathbf{U}_{ic+j,\,k}$ (coefficients / loadings).
We implement \textbf{mode1} (reconstruct-then-convolve):
$\hat{\W} = \sum_k \avec_k \otimes \psivec_k$, then \texttt{F.conv2d}.

Parameter counts per layer:

\textbf{Fourier-Bessel (FB) bases.}
For $l=3$, the 2-D Helmholtz equation on the unit disk yields
exactly \textbf{6} valid orthogonal basis functions ordered by spatial
frequency.  These are fixed (data-agnostic) and serve as a physically
motivated alternative to learned atoms.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:p1_setup}

\textbf{Architecture.}
We adapt AlexNet \cite{krizhevsky2012imagenet} to
$1{\times}32{\times}32$ grayscale MNIST (resized from $28{\times}28$):
five $3{\times}3$ conv layers (32, 64, 128, 128, 64 channels),
three max-pooling layers, and three fully-connected layers
($1024\to512\to256\to10$), with BatchNorm after each conv.

\textbf{PCA (Task 1).}
The baseline AlexNet is trained to convergence
(SGD, lr=0.01, cosine annealing, 30 epochs, batch size 128).
We then apply SVD to each layer's filter matrix and reconstruct
with $K = 1, \ldots, 9$ components \emph{without retraining}.

\textbf{DCF / FB (Task 2).}
Every \texttt{Conv2d} is replaced by \texttt{Conv\_DCF} (mode1).
DCF: $K \in \{1,\ldots,9\}$, \texttt{bases\_grad=True}, random init,
fully retrained.  FB: $K \in \{1,\ldots,6\}$, fixed analytical atoms,
only coefficients $a_k$ trained.

\textbf{Denoising Autoencoder (Task 3).}
A three-layer convolutional encoder ($1\to32\to64\to64$)
with BatchNorm and MaxPool, followed by three ConvTranspose2d decoder
layers restoring $1{\times}28{\times}28$.  Trained on MNIST corrupted
by additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma=0.3$, clipped), MSE loss, Adam.
DCF and FB variants replace the encoder Conv2d layers.
Metric: PSNR between reconstructed and clean images.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p1_disc}

\textbf{Basis choice matters more than compression ratio.}
The PCA experiment shows that \emph{which subspace} is used is critical,
not just \emph{how many dimensions} are kept.
At $K=6$, PCA (no retrain) gives 85.9\%, while DCF and FB both give
$>$99.6\% --- a 13.7-point gap despite identical parameter counts.
The difference is that DCF and FB optimise or analyse-from-scratch the
atoms jointly with the task, while PCA inherits atoms from a
\emph{different optimisation} (the full-rank weight training).

\textbf{Low effective rank of the filter manifold.}
The explained-variance curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:p1_ev}) show that
$K=5$ atoms capture $62$--$74\%$ of filter variance across layers.
The remaining $26$--$38\%$ is enough to destroy classification
without retraining, but can be discarded once the network is trained
with a compressed parameterisation from the start.

\textbf{FB as a principled alternative to learned atoms.}
FB atoms are derived from the 2-D Helmholtz equation and require no
training.  Their performance is indistinguishable from learned DCF atoms
on MNIST (Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc}), suggesting that natural image
statistics and digit manifolds are well aligned with low-frequency
spatial modes --- a well-known result in image processing that DCF
provides a neural-network perspective on.

\section{Part 2 --- Domain Adaptation via Selective DCF Freezing}
\label{sec:part2}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:p2_form}

\textbf{Domain shift.}
A classifier trained on MNIST (handwritten digits, white-on-black,
$1{\times}32{\times}32$) fails on SVHN (house-number photographs, colour,
real-world backgrounds).  We convert SVHN to grayscale by channel
averaging: $I_\text{gray}(x,y) = [I_R+I_G+I_B]/3$, making both
datasets structurally identical inputs ($1{\times}32{\times}32$) while
retaining the distribution gap.

\textbf{Adaptation hypothesis.}
The DCF decomposition $\W[i,j] = \sum_k a_k[i,j]\,\psivec_k$ separates
two types of information:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\psivec_k$ (\textbf{atoms}): low-level spatial primitives
        (edges, frequencies, blobs).
  \item $a_k$ (\textbf{coefficients}): how much each primitive
        contributes to each filter --- the \emph{functional recipe}.
\end{itemize}
We test two competing hypotheses:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt]
  \item[Exp B (``atoms are domain-specific''):]
        Freeze $a_k$, update $\psivec_k$ on SVHN.
        Hypothesis: semantics reside in $a_k$, appearance in $\psivec_k$.
  \item[Exp C (``coefficients are domain-specific''):]
        Freeze $\psivec_k$, update $a_k$ on SVHN.
        Hypothesis: atoms are universal primitives; re-weighting them
        suffices to adapt.
\end{description}
We also run \textbf{Exp A} (train DCF from scratch on SVHN, upper bound)
and \textbf{Task 1} (no adaptation, lower bound).

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:p2_setup}

\textbf{Stage 1} (source): Train DCF-AlexNet on MNIST (SGD, lr=0.01,
momentum 0.9, cosine annealing, 30 epochs).
\textbf{Stage 2} (adaptation): Fine-tune on SVHN (SGD, lr=0.001, 15
epochs) with the appropriate parameters frozen.
$K$ is swept over $\{1, 2, 4, 6, 8\}$.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p2_disc}

\textbf{The atoms are the domain-invariant component.}
The experimental outcome --- Exp C (93.3\%) $\gg$ Exp B (81.2\%) ---
\emph{reverses the original hypothesis} that atoms are domain-specific.
For digit-recognition domains, MNIST-learned spatial primitives
(edge detectors, frequency responses) are already well-suited to SVHN.
What must change is how those primitives are combined
(the coefficients $a_k$) to handle SVHN's richer texture,
variable backgrounds, and colour-averaging artifacts.

\textbf{Three complementary reasons:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{Shared low-level structure.}
        Both domains contain digit strokes with broadly similar
        local edge and frequency content.  The atoms encode this
        shared vocabulary; they do not need to change.

  \item \emph{Parameter budget asymmetry.}
        Exp B updates $K \times l^2 = 72$ atom parameters per layer
        (360 total).  Exp C updates $K \times c' \times c$ coefficient
        parameters per layer, totalling $\sim$279{,}000 across the
        network.  However, the 800$\times$ parameter advantage alone
        does not explain the result: Exp A (full retraining,
        $\sim$310{,}000 conv params) achieves only 1.2\% more than Exp C,
        confirming that frozen atoms are a genuine bottleneck for Exp B.

  \item \emph{Stable coefficient initialisation.}
        The MNIST-trained $a_k$ already encode \emph{which atomic
        combinations} produce class-discriminative responses.
        Fine-tuning from this starting point on SVHN is a small
        adjustment.  Exp B keeps the recipes ($a_k$) fixed but changes
        the ingredients ($\psivec_k$), disrupting the coherence built
        during MNIST training.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Comparison with the ACDA literature.}
Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2020dictionary} treat atoms as domain-specific
in an object-recognition setting (Office, VisDA), motivating Exp B.
Our result is not a contradiction: in object recognition, low-level
texture and colour statistics change dramatically between domains,
making atom adaptation important.  For digit domains, both source and
target share stroke-level primitives, and the domain shift is primarily
in higher-level statistics captured by $a_k$.

\textbf{Atom visualisation.}
Fig.~\ref{fig:p2_atoms} shows that after Exp B, atoms retain their
spatial frequency ordering but shift amplitude patterns, consistent with
SVHN's richer photographic content.  The fact that Exp B still
achieves 81.2\% (vs 18.2\% no-adapt) confirms that even atom-level
adaptation is useful --- just not as efficient as coefficient adaptation.

\section{Part 3 --- Continual Deblurring via DCF Coefficient Swapping}
\label{sec:part3}

\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:p3_motivation}

\textbf{Catastrophic forgetting in deployed systems.}
When a neural network is sequentially trained on tasks $T_0, T_1, \ldots$,
gradient updates for $T_t$ overwrite the weights learned for $T_j < t$,
causing severe performance degradation on earlier tasks
--- \emph{catastrophic forgetting} \cite{french1999catastrophic}.
The na\"{i}ve remedy (one model per task) incurs memory linear in the number
of tasks, which is infeasible for systems that encounter many incremental
conditions.

\textbf{Connection to image deblurring.}
My personal research focuses on \textbf{image deblurring for spatially
varying point spread functions (SV-PSFs)}.  A camera deployed in the
field encounters many blur conditions over its lifetime
(focus distance, aperture, atmospheric turbulence, sensor aging).
Each condition can be viewed as a new \emph{deblurring task}.
A practical system must deblur under new conditions without forgetting
previously mastered ones, and without storing a separate network per
blur type.

\textbf{Our approach: continual deblurring via coefficient swapping.}
We propose a DCF-based denoising autoencoder that separates filters into:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Shared atoms $\psivec_k$}: a universal dictionary of
        spatial deconvolution operators, learned once on Task~0 and
        frozen permanently.
  \item \textbf{Task-specific coefficients $\avec_k^{(t)}$}: a per-task
        snapshot encoding how the dictionary is combined for blur type $t$.
\end{itemize}
At inference time for blur condition $t$, loading $\avec_k^{(t)}$ and
combining with the fixed $\psivec_k$ \emph{exactly restores} the model
--- zero forgetting by construction.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:p3_form}

\textbf{Blur tasks.}  We define three sequential deblurring tasks using
synthetic PSFs applied to MNIST digits:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Synthetic blur tasks.}
\label{tab:p3_tasks}
\begin{tabular}{clll}
\toprule
Task $t$ & Blur type & PSF & Parameters \\
\midrule
0 & Gaussian (isotropic) & $h \propto e^{-\|\mathbf{r}\|^2/2\sigma^2}$ & $\sigma{=}1.5$, $7{\times}7$ \\
1 & Motion (linear)      & 1-px line at angle $\alpha$ & $\alpha{=}45^\circ$, $5$\,px \\
2 & Defocus (disk)       & $h = \mathbbm{1}[\|\mathbf{r}\| \le R]$ & $R{=}2.5$\,px, $7{\times}7$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{DCF-CL training protocol.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Task~0:} Train both $\psivec_k$ and $\avec_k^{(0)}$.
        After convergence, \textbf{freeze} $\psivec_k$. Store $\avec_k^{(0)}$.
  \item \textbf{Task $t > 0$:} Re-initialise task-specific parameters
        ($\avec_k^{(t)}$, BN, decoder).  Train only these while $\psivec_k$
        remains frozen.  Store $\avec_k^{(t)}$.
  \item \textbf{Inference on task $j$:} Inject $\avec_k^{(j)}$ into the
        model.  Because $\psivec_k$ is unchanged since Task~0, this is an
        exact restore --- zero forgetting.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Forgetting metric.}  Backward transfer (BWT):
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BWT} = \frac{1}{T{-}1}\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}
  \bigl(\mathrm{PSNR}_{T-1,\,t-1} - \mathrm{PSNR}^*_{t-1}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{PSNR}^*_j$ is the peak PSNR on Task~$j$ immediately after
its own training and $\mathrm{PSNR}_{t,j}$ is PSNR on Task~$j$ after
training through Task~$t$.  Negative BWT indicates forgetting.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:p3_setup}

Both the baseline and DCF-CL model use the same autoencoder architecture
(see Part 1): three-layer Conv/DCF encoder, three ConvTranspose decoder
layers.  The DCF-CL encoder uses $K=6$ atoms with mode1 reconstruction.
Both are trained with Adam (lr=$10^{-3}$, cosine annealing, 20 epochs per
task, batch size 128).  The baseline is a standard Conv autoencoder trained
sequentially on Tasks 0$\to$1$\to$2 without any CL mechanism.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p3_disc}

\textbf{Zero forgetting by construction.}
The coefficient-swapping mechanism provides an \emph{exact} restore of
any previously trained task: because the atoms $\psivec_k$ are frozen,
injecting $\avec_k^{(j)}$ recovers precisely the filter configuration
that was optimal for Task~$j$.  This is not an approximation --- it is
algebraically guaranteed.  The PSNR heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:p3_heatmaps})
confirms this: each column of the DCF-CL matrix is constant to four
decimal places.

\textbf{Honest assessment of memory savings.}
With $K=6$ atoms and our shallow autoencoder, the shared atoms constitute
only 162 out of 93,315 model parameters (0.17\%).  The per-task snapshot
in our implementation stores DCF coefficients, BN running statistics,
and the full decoder ($\sim$93,670 params per task), nearly the same as
storing a full model.  As a result, DCF-CL achieves only a modest
1.19$\times$ compression relative to the Conv baseline, and \emph{no}
compression relative to storing three DCF models of the same size.

The memory advantage of DCF-CL becomes more pronounced as:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $K$ increases (atoms are a larger fraction of encoder parameters),
  \item The network deepens (more layers share the same $K$ atoms),
  \item The decoder is also shared across tasks (reducing per-task footprint
        from $\sim$93K to $\sim$37K for just the $a_k$ tensors, yielding a
        genuine $\sim$2.5$\times$ compression over three DCF models).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Connection to SV-PSF research.}
The continual deblurring framework maps naturally to spatially varying PSF
correction:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{Diverse blur conditions over time:} Each new calibration
        state (focus distance, optical configuration) becomes a new task.
        The shared atoms form a universal deconvolution dictionary valid
        across all states; only the lightweight per-state coefficients
        are updated.
  \item \emph{Spatially varying extension:} The coefficient-swapping
        mechanism could be extended to a \emph{spatially varying
        coefficient map} $\avec_k^{(x,y)}$, where each image patch uses
        its own coefficient set while sharing global atoms --- a spatially
        adaptive deconvolution network without per-pixel full models.
  \item \emph{Online adaptation:} When a new PSF condition is detected,
        the system fine-tunes only $\avec_k^{(\text{new})}$ (with atoms
        frozen), which converges far faster than retraining a full network.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Limitations.}
The atoms are learned only on Task~0 (Gaussian blur).  If subsequent
blur types require spatial frequencies not well represented by
Gaussian-trained atoms, performance may be suboptimal.
Task~1 (29.38\,dB) and Task~2 (29.48\,dB) already exceed Task~0
(28.89\,dB) despite using Task~0's atoms, suggesting the atoms are
sufficiently expressive --- but this cannot be guaranteed for all
PSF types.  Future work could grow the atom set incrementally
\cite{miao2021continual}.

\section{Unified Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The three parts of this project form a coherent story about the
\textbf{low-dimensional subspace structure of CNN convolutional filters}:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt]
  \item[Part 1 reveals the structure.]
        Learned filters occupy a low-rank subspace: $K \le 6$ atoms
        (out of 9 possible for $3{\times}3$ kernels) suffice for
        near-perfect classification and denoising when the basis is
        optimised.  PCA on pre-trained filters exposes this structure
        post-hoc; DCF and FB exploit it during training.

  \item[Part 2 exploits the structure for transfer.]
        The DCF decomposition separates domain-invariant atoms from
        domain-specific coefficients.  Counterintuitively --- and
        contrary to the original ACDA hypothesis --- for digit-domain
        transfer (MNIST$\to$SVHN), the \emph{atoms} are the transferable
        component and only the \emph{coefficients} need to be re-learned.
        Adapting coefficients alone (Exp C) achieves 93.3\% SVHN
        accuracy, within 1.2\% of full retraining.

  \item[Part 3 exploits the structure for continual learning.]
        Freezing atoms after the first task and storing only per-task
        coefficients yields exact zero-forgetting continual learning.
        The shared atoms define a universal deconvolution dictionary;
        each new blur condition is handled by a lightweight coefficient
        snapshot.  This connects directly to SV-PSF research, where the
        same mechanism supports efficient on-device multi-condition
        deblurring without model proliferation.
\end{description}

\noindent
Together, these results validate the central hypothesis: \emph{the
low-rank subspace structure of CNN filters is not merely a compression
curiosity --- it is the key architectural principle enabling compression,
domain transfer, and continual learning within a single unified
parameterisation.}
