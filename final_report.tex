%% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
This report presents a unified study of the \emph{Decomposed Convolutional
Filter} (DCF) framework across three parts.
\textbf{Part 1} shows that learned CNN filters occupy a remarkably
low-dimensional subspace: PCA truncation requires $K \ge 7$ of 9 components
to preserve accuracy without retraining, yet a single learned DCF atom
($K=1$) achieves 98.96\% on MNIST, and Fourier-Bessel (FB) bases match
this performance with no data-driven atom learning.
\textbf{Part 2} applies DCF to MNIST$\to$SVHN domain adaptation.
Counterintuitively, adapting coefficient matrices $a_k$ while keeping
atoms frozen (Exp~C, 93.3\% at $K=8$) far outperforms adapting atoms
while keeping coefficients frozen (Exp~B, 81.2\%), revealing that atoms
are the \emph{domain-invariant} component for digit domains.
\textbf{Part 3} frames continual deblurring as a coefficient-swapping
problem: shared atoms define a universal deconvolution dictionary, and
per-task coefficient snapshots achieve zero catastrophic forgetting
(backward transfer $= 0$\,dB vs.\ $-13.1$\,dB for the naive baseline).
Together, the three parts demonstrate that the low-rank subspace structure
of CNN filters enables compression, adaptation, and continual learning
within a single unified parameterisation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Part 1 --- PCA View of CNN Filters}
\label{sec:part1}

\noindent
\textbf{Background: Decomposed Convolutional Filters (DCF).}
Let $\W \in \R^{c' \times c \times l \times l}$ be a conv layer weight tensor.
Reshaping to $\W' \in \R^{c'c \times l^2}$ and applying the compact SVD gives
\begin{equation}
  \W' = \mathbf{U}\mathbf{S}\mathbf{V}^\top, \qquad
  \mathbf{U}\in\R^{c'c\times r},\;
  \mathbf{S}=\mathrm{diag}(s_1,\ldots,s_r),\;
  \mathbf{V}\in\R^{l^2\times r}.
\end{equation}
Truncating to $K$ components gives the DCF representation \cite{qiu2018dcfnet}:
\begin{equation}
  \W[i,j] \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} a_k[i,j]\,\psivec_k,
  \qquad
  \psivec_k \in \R^{l \times l},\quad a_k \in \R^{c' \times c},
  \label{eq:dcf}
\end{equation}
where $\psivec_k$ are \textbf{filter atoms} and $a_k$ are \textbf{coefficients}.
We use \textbf{mode1} (reconstruct-then-convolve):
$\hat{\W} = \sum_k \avec_k \otimes \psivec_k$, then \texttt{F.conv2d}.
Parameter counts are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:p1_params}.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Parameter count per conv layer ($c$ input, $c'$ output,
         kernel $l{\times}l$, $K$ atoms).}
\label{tab:p1_params}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Variant & Learnable atoms? & Parameters \\
\midrule
Standard Conv2d     & ---        & $c' c l^2$ \\

FB (fixed atoms)    & $\times$   & $c' c K$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent
\textbf{Architecture.}
AlexNet \cite{krizhevsky2012imagenet} is adapted to $1{\times}32{\times}32$
grayscale MNIST: five $3{\times}3$ conv layers (32, 64, 128, 128, 64 channels)
with BatchNorm, three max-pooling layers, and FC layers
$1024\to512\to256\to10$.
DCF and FB variants replace every \texttt{Conv2d} with \texttt{Conv\_DCF}
(mode1).

\subsection{Task 1: PCA Filter Truncation}
\label{sec:p1_task1}

The first task investigates the intrinsic dimensionality of trained CNN
filters by applying PCA-based truncation to a fully trained baseline model.
The central question is: \emph{how many principal components are needed to
preserve classification accuracy when no retraining is allowed?}

\textbf{Setup.}
A baseline AlexNet is first trained to convergence on MNIST using SGD with
learning rate 0.01, cosine annealing, for 30 epochs with batch size 128.
After training, we apply SVD to each convolutional layer's reshaped filter
matrix and reconstruct the filters using only $K = 1, \ldots, 9$ principal
components \emph{without any retraining}, measuring the resulting test
accuracy at each $K$.  In parallel, we also train DCF-AlexNet models
(with learned atoms) end-to-end for each $K$ to compare the effect of
post-hoc truncation versus joint optimisation.

\textbf{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:p1_task1} presents the main Task~1 findings.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:p1_acc}, the PCA curve (blue, no retraining)
exhibits a dramatic accuracy cliff: for $K \le 4$, accuracy remains at
chance level (9.74\%), barely improves to 26.39\% at $K=5$, then jumps to
85.92\% at $K=6$ and 97.0\% at $K=7$.  Only the full-rank reconstruction
($K=9$) restores the 99.65\% baseline.  In stark contrast, the DCF curve
(orange, retrained end-to-end) shows that a single learned atom ($K=1$)
already achieves 98.96\%, and $K \ge 2$ matches or exceeds the full-rank
baseline.  This gap reveals that the penalty for compression vanishes
entirely when the basis is jointly optimised with the classification
objective.  The corresponding parameter count plot
(Figure~\ref{fig:p1_params}) confirms that the conv parameter budget
grows linearly in $K$, so any $K < 9$ yields genuine compression below the
full-rank baseline.

Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc} provides the full numerical comparison across all
three methods.  The DCF column demonstrates remarkably stable performance
across all $K$, while the PCA column's sharp cliff underscores the
fragility of post-hoc truncation.

To understand why the PCA curve exhibits such a sharp cliff, we examine
the cumulative explained variance per layer.  As shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:p1_ev}, Conv1 reaches 83\% explained variance at $K=6$
and 95.5\% at $K=8$, while deeper layers saturate even more slowly
(only 73\% at $K=6$).  All layers require $K=9$ for 100\% variance.
This is consistent with the accuracy cliff: even 5--17\% of unexplained
variance is sufficient to destroy the classification signal when no
retraining is performed, because the discarded components encode
task-relevant structure that the downstream layers depend upon.

\subsection{Task 2: Fourier-Bessel Bases vs Learned DCF Atoms}
\label{sec:p1_task2}

Task~1 established that jointly optimised atoms greatly outperform post-hoc
PCA truncation.  A natural follow-up question is whether the atoms
\emph{need} to be learned at all, or whether a fixed analytical basis can
perform equally well.  Fourier-Bessel (FB) functions --- the radial
eigenfunctions of the 2-D Helmholtz equation on the unit disk --- form a
natural candidate basis because they span the same low-frequency spatial
modes that CNN filters typically learn.

\textbf{Setup.}
We compare two DCF variants, both retrained from scratch on MNIST:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{DCF (learned):} $K \in \{1,\ldots,9\}$,
        \texttt{bases\_grad=True}, random atom initialisation.
        Both atoms $\psivec_k$ and coefficients $a_k$ are optimised.
  \item \textbf{FB (fixed):} $K \in \{1,\ldots,6\}$, atoms set to the
        6 Fourier-Bessel bases (the maximum number available for a
        $3{\times}3$ kernel), \texttt{bases\_grad=False}.
        Only coefficients $a_k$ are trained.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:p1_task2} compares the accuracy of FB, DCF, and PCA across
varying $K$.  As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:p1_fb_dcf}, the FB and DCF
curves closely track each other across all overlapping values of $K$.
Both methods surpass 99.4\% accuracy at $K=2$, far exceeding PCA's 9.74\%
at the same compression level.  Notably, FB reaches 99.64\% at its maximum
of $K=6$, which matches the best DCF accuracy of 99.64\% at $K=8$.  This
near-perfect agreement indicates that the analytical FB subspace spans
essentially the same task-relevant directions as the data-driven atoms
learned by DCF.

Figure~\ref{fig:p1_fb_atoms} displays the 6 FB bases, ordered from a DC
blob ($k=1$) to progressively finer oscillatory patterns ($k=6$).  This
frequency ordering mirrors the spectral content typically observed in
trained CNN filters.  To illustrate this correspondence, Figure~\ref{fig:p1_pca_atoms}
shows the top PCA atoms extracted from Conv1 of the trained baseline AlexNet.
The first few principal components ($\psivec_1$--$\psivec_3$) resemble
low-frequency edge detectors, while higher components encode finer spatial
structure.  The visual similarity between these learned atoms and the
analytical FB bases in Figure~\ref{fig:p1_fb_atoms} explains why fixed FB
atoms can match fully learned DCF atoms: the filter manifold of digit
classifiers is naturally well-aligned with the Fourier-Bessel subspace.

The full numerical comparison is included in Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc} (FB
column).  At every $K$ from 1 to 6, FB accuracy is within 0.6\% of the
corresponding DCF value, confirming that data-driven atom learning provides
negligible benefit for this task.

\subsection{Task 3: Denoising Autoencoder with DCF Encoder}
\label{sec:p1_task3}

Tasks~1 and~2 focused on classification.  Task~3 tests whether the
low-rank filter structure extends to a different objective ---
image denoising --- by building a convolutional autoencoder whose encoder
uses DCF or FB layers.

\textbf{Setup.}
The autoencoder comprises a three-layer encoder
($1\to32\to64\to64$, BatchNorm, MaxPool) and a symmetric three-layer
\texttt{ConvTranspose2d} decoder that restores the $1{\times}28{\times}28$
output.  The model is trained on MNIST images corrupted by additive
Gaussian noise ($\sigma=0.3$, clipped to $[0,1]$) using MSE loss, Adam
optimiser, and 30 epochs.  DCF and FB variants replace the encoder's
\texttt{Conv2d} layers with \texttt{Conv\_DCF}, while the decoder remains
standard.  We report PSNR (dB) between the reconstructed and clean images
on the held-out test set.

\textbf{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:p1_psnr} plots PSNR versus $K$ for both DCF and FB
autoencoders.  Both curves rise steeply from $K=1$ to $K=3$, then plateau
as additional atoms provide diminishing returns.  The DCF encoder reaches
23.75\,dB at $K=9$, while the FB encoder reaches 23.71\,dB at its maximum
of $K=6$ --- nearly identical ceilings despite FB having fewer available
atoms.  Even at $K=4$, DCF achieves 23.58\,dB, within 0.17\,dB of its
asymptotic ceiling.  These results confirm that the low-rank subspace
structure observed in classification filters also holds for denoising
filters: a small number of atoms captures essentially all of the
task-relevant spatial information.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p1_disc}

The results from Tasks~1--3 collectively reveal several important
properties of the CNN filter subspace.

\textbf{Basis choice matters more than compression ratio.}
Comparing the three methods at the same $K$ in Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc}
highlights a striking finding: at $K=6$, PCA (no retrain) gives 85.9\%
while DCF and FB both give $>$99.6\% --- a 13.7-point gap at
\emph{identical parameter counts}.  The crucial difference is that DCF and
FB optimise the atom-coefficient decomposition jointly with the task
objective, whereas PCA inherits its basis from a different optimisation
(full-rank training) and thus retains principal components that are
energetically dominant but not necessarily task-relevant.

\textbf{Low effective rank of the filter manifold.}
The explained-variance curves in Figure~\ref{fig:p1_ev} reveal that $K=5$
captures only 62--74\% of filter variance, depending on the layer.  The
remaining 26--38\% is sufficient to destroy classification accuracy when
no retraining is allowed, yet this same unexplained variance is
\emph{insignificant} when the network is trained end-to-end with the
compressed parameterisation (as seen in the DCF and FB columns of
Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc}).  This indicates that the energy in the discarded
PCA components is redistributed during joint training, and the network
learns to concentrate its representational capacity into the available
$K$ dimensions.

\textbf{FB as a principled alternative.}
As shown in Figures~\ref{fig:p1_fb_dcf} and~\ref{fig:p1_psnr}, FB atoms
require no data-driven learning and are indistinguishable from learned DCF
atoms on both classification (Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc}, within 0.6\% at all
$K$) and denoising (23.71 vs 23.75\,dB at maximum $K$).  The visual
comparison of FB bases (Figure~\ref{fig:p1_fb_atoms}) with PCA-extracted
atoms (Figure~\ref{fig:p1_pca_atoms}) confirms that the filter manifold
of digit classifiers is naturally well-aligned with low-frequency
analytical spatial modes.  This makes FB a compelling choice in
applications where atom learning is impractical or where a fixed,
interpretable basis is preferred.

\section{Part 2 --- Domain Adaptation via Selective DCF Freezing}
\label{sec:part2}

\noindent
\textbf{Background.}
Part~1 established that CNN filters lie in a low-dimensional subspace that
can be efficiently parameterised by the DCF decomposition.  Part~2 asks
whether this decomposition can also facilitate \emph{domain adaptation}:
can a model trained on one digit dataset be efficiently adapted to a
different one by selectively updating only part of the DCF representation?

The source domain is MNIST \cite{lecun1998gradient} (handwritten digits,
white-on-black), and the target is SVHN \cite{netzer2011reading}
(house-number photographs, colour, real-world backgrounds).  SVHN images
are converted to grayscale by channel averaging
($I_\text{gray} = (I_R+I_G+I_B)/3$), making both datasets share the same
$1{\times}32{\times}32$ input format while retaining a substantial
distribution gap in appearance, background clutter, and contrast.

The DCF decomposition $\W[i,j] = \sum_k a_k[i,j]\,\psivec_k$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:dcf})
naturally separates the filter into two distinct components:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\psivec_k$ (\textbf{atoms}): low-level spatial primitives that
        define the dictionary of spatial patterns.
  \item $a_k$ (\textbf{coefficients}): the task-specific recipe that
        determines how atoms are linearly combined for each filter.
\end{itemize}
This separation enables a controlled experiment: by selectively freezing
one component and adapting the other, we can probe which part of the
representation carries domain-specific versus domain-invariant information.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Task 1: Baseline --- No Adaptation}
\label{sec:p2_task1}

Before attempting any adaptation, we first quantify the raw domain gap by
evaluating a model trained entirely on the source domain.

\textbf{Setup.}
The standard Conv2d AlexNet (same architecture as Part~1) is trained on
MNIST using SGD with learning rate 0.01, momentum 0.9, cosine annealing,
for 30 epochs, and then evaluated directly on the SVHN test set without
any fine-tuning or modification.

\textbf{Result.}
The MNIST-trained model achieves only \textbf{18.2\%} accuracy on SVHN,
compared with 99.65\% on MNIST --- an 81-point drop that sharply quantifies
the domain gap.  This near-chance performance (chance on SVHN's 10 classes
is 10\%) confirms that the distribution shift between handwritten and
street-view digits is severe enough to render naive transfer ineffective,
and motivates the selective adaptation strategies explored in Task~2.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Task 2: DCF Adaptation Experiments}
\label{sec:p2_task2}

\textbf{Setup.}
Three experiments use DCF-AlexNet with $K \in \{1,2,4,6,8\}$:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt]
  \item[Exp A (upper bound):] Train DCF from scratch on SVHN.
        This serves as the performance ceiling, since the model has full
        access to the target domain during training.
  \item[Exp B (adapt atoms):] Stage~1: train DCF on MNIST
        (\texttt{bases\_grad=True}, 30 epochs, lr=0.01).
        Stage~2: freeze $a_k$, unfreeze $\psivec_k$, fine-tune on SVHN
        (15 epochs, lr=0.001).
        This tests the hypothesis that atoms are domain-specific and must
        be adapted.
  \item[Exp C (adapt coefficients):] Same Stage~1 as Exp~B, then
        freeze $\psivec_k$, unfreeze $a_k$, fine-tune on SVHN.
        This tests the complementary hypothesis that coefficients carry the
        domain-specific information.
\end{description}

\textbf{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:p2_main} summarises the key comparison at $K=8$.  The most
striking finding is that \textbf{Exp~C (93.3\%)} vastly outperforms
\textbf{Exp~B (81.2\%)}, a 12.1-point gap that is opposite to the initial
hypothesis that atoms would be the domain-specific component requiring
adaptation.  Exp~C also comes within just 1.2\% of the Exp~A upper bound
(94.5\%), demonstrating that adapting only the coefficient matrices is
sufficient to close nearly the entire domain gap.  The pre-adapt baseline
(20.3\%) is barely above the no-adaptation Conv baseline (18.2\%),
confirming that the DCF parameterisation alone does not resolve the
distribution shift.

Table~\ref{tab:p2_vsK} presents the full accuracy breakdown across all
values of $K$.  The advantage of Exp~C over Exp~B is consistent at every
$K \ge 2$ and widens as $K$ increases, reaching its maximum at $K=8$.
Exp~A saturates at approximately 94.5\% for $K \ge 4$, suggesting that
four atoms are sufficient to capture the spatial filter structure needed
for SVHN classification.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p2_disc}

The experimental results from Task~2 yield a clear and somewhat
counterintuitive conclusion about the roles of atoms and coefficients in
domain adaptation.

\textbf{The atoms are the domain-invariant component.}
As shown in Tables~\ref{tab:p2_main} and~\ref{tab:p2_vsK} and visualised
in Figure~\ref{fig:p2_acc_vs_K}, Exp~C (93.3\%) vastly outperforms Exp~B
(81.2\%), a result that \emph{reverses the original hypothesis} that atoms
encode domain-specific spatial patterns requiring adaptation.
Instead, for digit domains, MNIST-learned spatial primitives are already
well-suited to SVHN; what must change is how these primitives are
\emph{combined} through the coefficient matrices $a_k$.  The atom
visualisation in Figure~\ref{fig:p2_atoms} supports this interpretation:
the MNIST and SVHN atoms share the same low-to-high frequency ordering,
indicating that the spatial dictionary transfers across domains with
minimal adjustment.

\textbf{Three contributing factors explain this finding:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{Shared low-level structure.}  Both MNIST and SVHN contain
        digit strokes with similar local edge orientations and frequency
        content.  The atoms encode this shared visual vocabulary, while the
        coefficients encode the domain-specific weighting that adapts the
        vocabulary to each domain's particular appearance statistics.

  \item \emph{Parameter budget asymmetry.}  Exp~B updates only 72 atom
        parameters per layer (360 total across 5 layers); Exp~C updates
        $\sim$279{,}000 coefficient parameters.  Yet Exp~A (full
        retraining, $\sim$310{,}000 parameters) achieves only 1.2\% more
        than Exp~C, confirming that the frozen atoms are a genuine capacity
        bottleneck for Exp~B --- not merely a parameter-count disadvantage.

  \item \emph{Stable initialisation from source training.}  The
        MNIST-trained coefficients $a_k$ already encode which atomic
        combinations produce class-discriminative feature maps.
        Fine-tuning these coefficients on SVHN is a comparatively small
        adjustment.  Exp~B, by contrast, perturbs the spatial ingredients
        while keeping the recipes fixed, which is a fundamentally more
        disruptive change.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Comparison with the ACDA literature.}
Wang et al.\ \cite{wang2020dictionary} treat atoms as domain-specific in
object-recognition benchmarks (e.g., VisDA, Office-Home) where low-level
texture changes dramatically between domains (photographs vs.\ sketches
vs.\ clipart).  For digit domains, however, both source and target share
stroke-level primitives, so the domain shift is captured in the
higher-level coefficient structure rather than in the spatial dictionary
itself.  This suggests that the optimal adaptation strategy (atoms vs.\
coefficients) depends on the \emph{nature} of the domain gap, and our
results complement the ACDA findings by identifying a regime where
coefficient adaptation alone is sufficient.

\section{Part 3 --- Continual Deblurring via DCF Coefficient Swapping}
\label{sec:part3}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Motivation and Connection to SV-PSF Research}
\label{sec:p3_motivation}

Parts~1 and~2 demonstrated that the DCF decomposition enables compression
and domain adaptation by separating filters into shared atoms and
task-specific coefficients.  Part~3 extends this idea to the
\emph{continual learning} setting, where a model must sequentially learn
multiple tasks without forgetting previously acquired knowledge.

Sequential training of neural networks is known to cause
\emph{catastrophic forgetting} \cite{french1999catastrophic}: gradient
updates optimised for the current task overwrite weights that were critical
for earlier tasks, leading to severe performance degradation on previously
learned objectives.  The na\"{i}ve fix --- maintaining one independent
model per task --- avoids forgetting but incurs memory cost that grows
linearly with the number of tasks, which is impractical for large-scale
deployment.

My personal research on \textbf{image deblurring for spatially varying
PSFs (SV-PSFs)} makes this problem directly relevant.
A deployed camera encounters many different blur conditions over its
lifetime (varying focus distance, aperture setting, atmospheric
turbulence, sensor aging); each condition constitutes a distinct
deblurring task.  A practical system must master new blur conditions
without forgetting how to handle old ones, and without storing a
separate CNN per blur type.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Problem Formulation and Approach}
\label{sec:p3_form}

\textbf{Blur tasks.}
We define three sequential synthetic deblurring tasks on MNIST, each
applying a different point spread function (PSF) to simulate a distinct
blur condition.  The tasks are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:p3_tasks}
and visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:p3_blur}.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Three sequential synthetic deblurring tasks.}
\label{tab:p3_tasks}
\begin{tabular}{clll}
\toprule
Task $t$ & Blur type & PSF & Parameters \\
\midrule
0 & Gaussian  & $h \propto e^{-\|\mathbf{r}\|^2/2\sigma^2}$ & $\sigma{=}1.5$, $7{\times}7$ \\
1 & Motion    & 1-px line at angle $\alpha$ & $\alpha{=}45^\circ$, $5$\,px \\
2 & Defocus   & $h = \mathbbm{1}[\|\mathbf{r}\| \le R]$ & $R{=}2.5$\,px, $7{\times}7$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{DCF-CL approach.}
Building on the insight from Part~2 that atoms can remain frozen while
coefficients capture task-specific information, we propose a
coefficient-swapping strategy for continual learning.
A DCF-based denoising autoencoder separates filters into:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Shared atoms $\psivec_k$}: learned on Task~0, frozen permanently.
  \item \textbf{Task-specific coefficients $\avec_k^{(t)}$}: a per-task
        snapshot of how the dictionary is combined.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Protocol:}
(1)~Train all parameters on Task~0, then freeze $\psivec_k$ and store
$\avec_k^{(0)}$.
(2)~For each Task~$t > 0$: re-initialise $\avec_k^{(t)}$, BN, and decoder;
train only these while $\psivec_k$ stays frozen; store $\avec_k^{(t)}$.
(3)~Inference on Task~$j$: inject $\avec_k^{(j)}$ --- an \emph{exact} restore,
since $\psivec_k$ is unchanged.

\textbf{Forgetting metric.}  Backward transfer (BWT):
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{BWT} = \frac{1}{T{-}1}\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}
  \bigl(\mathrm{PSNR}_{T-1,\,t-1} - \mathrm{PSNR}^*_{t-1}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{PSNR}^*_j$ is peak PSNR on Task~$j$ right after its own
training.  Negative BWT $=$ forgetting; DCF-CL gives BWT $= 0$ by construction.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:p3_setup}

Both the baseline and DCF-CL models use the same autoencoder architecture
from Part~1 Task~3: a three-layer Conv/DCF encoder
($1\to32\to64\to64$, BatchNorm, MaxPool) and a symmetric three-layer
\texttt{ConvTranspose2d} decoder.  The DCF-CL variant uses $K=6$ atoms
(matching the Fourier-Bessel maximum for $3{\times}3$ kernels, as
established in Part~1).  Both models are trained with the Adam optimiser
(lr=$10^{-3}$), cosine annealing, for 20 epochs per task with batch size
128.

The \emph{baseline} is a standard Conv autoencoder trained sequentially
on Tasks~0$\to$1$\to$2 without any continual learning mechanism --- each
new task simply continues training from the previous task's final weights,
which is the na\"{i}ve approach that maximally exposes the model to
catastrophic forgetting.  The DCF-CL model follows the protocol described
in Section~\ref{sec:p3_form}: freeze atoms after Task~0, then train and
store per-task coefficients (along with BatchNorm and decoder states) for
each subsequent task.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:p3_disc}

\textbf{Zero forgetting by construction.}
The coefficient-swapping mechanism achieves zero forgetting not as an
approximation but as an algebraic guarantee: injecting $\avec_k^{(j)}$
with frozen $\psivec_k$ exactly recovers the Task-$j$ encoder, and the
per-task decoder and BatchNorm states are stored separately.  The PSNR
heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:p3_heatmaps} confirms this empirically: every
column of the DCF-CL matrix is constant to four decimal places across all
training phases.  This contrasts sharply with the baseline, where the
forgetting curves in Figure~\ref{fig:p3_forgetting} show catastrophic
PSNR degradation of up to 18.5\,dB on previously learned tasks.

\textbf{Honest assessment of memory savings.}
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:p3_memory}, the memory compression provided
by DCF-CL in this architecture is modest ($1.19\times$).  With $K=6$,
the shared atoms constitute only 162 of 93{,}315 total parameters
(0.17\%).  Per-task storage includes the DCF coefficients, BatchNorm
running statistics, and the full decoder ($\sim$93{,}670 parameters
per task), which is nearly equal to storing a complete model.  DCF-CL
thus achieves meaningful compression only relative to the Conv baseline,
and essentially no compression relative to storing three independent DCF
models of the same size.  The memory advantage would be substantially
larger in architectures where the decoder is also decomposed or shared
across tasks, or where a deeper or wider encoder is used (so that atoms
constitute a larger fraction of total parameters).

\textbf{Connection to SV-PSF research.}
The DCF-CL framework has direct implications for practical image
deblurring systems that must handle multiple blur conditions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{New conditions without forgetting:} each new PSF calibration
        state (e.g., a new focus distance or atmospheric condition) adds
        only a lightweight coefficient snapshot; the shared atom dictionary
        is reused, avoiding the need to retrain or store a separate model.
  \item \emph{Spatially varying extension:} a spatially varying coefficient
        map $\avec_k^{(x,y)}$ would enable per-patch adaptation while
        sharing global atoms --- effectively a spatially adaptive
        deconvolution network that handles position-dependent blur within
        a single forward pass.
  \item \emph{Online adaptation:} only $\avec_k^{(\text{new})}$ needs to
        be fine-tuned (atoms remain frozen), which converges much faster
        than full model retraining and is suitable for on-device deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Limitations.}
A key limitation of the current approach is that atoms are learned only
on Task~0 (Gaussian blur) and never updated thereafter.  If later tasks
require spatial frequencies or patterns absent from the Gaussian-trained
atom set, performance could degrade.  In our experiments, Tasks~1 and~2
achieve higher PSNR (29.38 and 29.48\,dB) than Task~0 (28.89\,dB),
as reported in Table~\ref{tab:p3_results}, suggesting that the $K=6$
atom dictionary is sufficiently expressive for these blur types.  However,
this cannot be guaranteed for all PSF types, particularly highly
anisotropic or structured blur kernels.  Future work could address this by
growing the atom set incrementally as new tasks arrive
\cite{miao2021continual}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The three parts of this project form a coherent narrative about the
\textbf{low-rank subspace structure of CNN filters} and its practical
implications:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt]
  \item[Part 1 reveals the structure.]
        As demonstrated in Table~\ref{tab:p1_acc} and
        Figure~\ref{fig:p1_task1}, the filter manifold of trained CNNs is
        remarkably low-dimensional: $K \le 6$ atoms suffice for
        near-perfect classification and denoising when the basis is jointly
        optimised.  PCA exposes this structure post-hoc but cannot exploit
        it without retraining; DCF and FB exploit it during training,
        achieving $>$99.4\% accuracy at $K=2$ where PCA gives only 9.7\%.
        The near-identical performance of fixed Fourier-Bessel bases and
        learned DCF atoms (Figure~\ref{fig:p1_fb_dcf}) further confirms
        that the relevant subspace is analytically predictable for digit
        classification.

  \item[Part 2 exploits the structure for transfer.]
        For digit-domain adaptation (MNIST$\to$SVHN), the experiments
        in Table~\ref{tab:p2_main} and Figure~\ref{fig:p2_acc_vs_K}
        reveal that atoms are the \emph{transferable} component while
        coefficients carry \emph{domain-specific} information.
        Adapting only $a_k$ (Exp~C) achieves 93.3\% --- within 1.2\% of
        full retraining --- while adapting only atoms (Exp~B) plateaus at
        81.2\%.

  \item[Part 3 exploits the structure for continual learning.]
        Freezing atoms and storing per-task coefficient snapshots yields
        exact zero-forgetting (BWT $= 0.0$\,dB), as confirmed by the
        constant columns in the PSNR heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:p3_heatmaps})
        and the flat forgetting curves (Figure~\ref{fig:p3_forgetting}).
        The mechanism extends naturally to SV-PSF deblurring, where
        per-condition coefficient snapshots enable on-device
        multi-condition deblurring without model proliferation.
\end{description}

\noindent
Together, these results validate that the low-rank subspace structure of
CNN filters is not merely a compression curiosity --- it is a
fundamental architectural principle that enables compression, domain
transfer, and continual learning within a single unified
parameterisation.  The DCF decomposition provides a principled and
interpretable framework for all three capabilities.
